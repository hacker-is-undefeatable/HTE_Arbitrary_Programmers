**Charge by rubbing (insulators)**

(a) Interaction between plastic rods rubbed on fur  
Plain plastic rods neither attract nor repel each other ...  
... but after being rubbed with fur, the rods repel each other.

(b) Interaction between glass rods rubbed on silk  
Plain glass rods neither attract nor repel each other ...  
... but after being rubbed with silk, the rods repel each other.

(c) Interaction between objects with opposite charges  
The fur-rubbed plastic rod and the silk-rubbed glass rod attract each other ...

---

**Charge by induction (conductors)**

(a) Uncharged metal ball  
(b) Negative charge on rod repels electrons, creating zones of negative and positive charge.  
(c) Wire lets electron buildup (induced negative charge) flow into ground.  
(d) Wire removed; ball now has only an electron-deficient region of positive charge.  
(e) Rod removed; electrons rearrange themselves, ball has overall electron deficiency (net positive charge).

---

**Real Life Examples**  
**Combing hair in winter**  

(a) How a negatively charged comb attracts an insulator  
Electrons in each molecule of the neutral insulator shift away from the comb.  
As a result, the $(+)$ charges in each molecule are closer to the comb than the $(−)$ charges and so feel a stronger force from the comb. Therefore the net force is attractive.

(b) How a positively charged comb attracts an insulator  
This time, electrons in the molecules shift toward the comb ...  
... so that the $(−)$ charges in each molecule are closer to the comb, and feel a stronger force from it than the $(+)$ charges. Again, the net force is attractive.

**Laser printing**
1. Laser beam "writes" on the drum, leaving negatively charged areas where the image will be.
2. Wire sprays ions onto drum, giving the drum a positive charge.
3. Roller applies positively charged toner to drum. Toner adheres only to negatively charged areas of the drum "written" by the laser.
4. Wires spray a stronger negative charge on paper so toner will adhere to it.

**Electrostatic painting**

Minimizes overspray and gives a smooth finish.

**Coulomb’s Law**

Magnitude of electric force between two point charges

$$
F = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2}
$$

**Vacuum permittivity**

$$
\epsilon_0 = 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2
$$

$$
\frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} = 8.988 \times 10^9 \, \text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{C}^2
$$

**Warning**: Two 1 C charge at 1 m apart exert a force of $9 \times 10^9$ N on each other! 1 C is a huge amount of electric charge!

Very much like gravitation, 

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

but much stronger.

E.g. two helium nuclei (He$^{2+}, \, m = 6.64 \times 10^{-27} \, \text{kg}$ and $q = 3.2 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{C}$), ratio of electric to gravitational force is

$$
\frac{F_e}{F_g} = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \cdot \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2} = 3.1 \times 10^{35}
$$

**Example 21.4 P. 717 Vector Addition of Electric Forces in a Plane**

Two equal positive charges $q_1 = q_2 = 2.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ are located at $x = 0, \, y = 0.30 \, \text{m}$ and $x = 0, \, y = -0.30 \, \text{m}$. Find the total electric force experienced by a charge $Q = 4.0 \, \mu\text{C}$ at $x = 0.40 \, \text{m}$ and $y = 0$?

By symmetry, $F_y = 0$

$$
F_x = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} \frac{|Q q_1|}{r^2} \cos \alpha
$$

$$
= 2 \cdot (9.0 \times 10^9 \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{C}^2) \cdot (4.0 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{C}) \cdot (2.0 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{C}) \cdot \frac{(0.40 \, \text{m})}{(0.50 \, \text{m})^2} = \ldots \, \text{N}
$$

**Warning**: be careful about the precision!

⚠️ Q feels the charge due to other charges ($q_1$ and $q_2$) only, never its own charge.  
⚠️ If charge Q changed, just substitute a new value into the above formula. Did $q_1$ and $q_2$ set up something independent of charge Q? Yes, they set up an electric field.

### Electric Field due to a Point Charge
Always radially outward (for +ve charge) or inward (for –ve charge)

- (a) The field produced by a positive point charge
- (b) The field produced by a negative point charge

$$ \mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_0} $$

**Electric field**  
electric force  
per unit charge

**Value of test charge**  
$$\mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2} \hat{\mathbf{r}} \quad (21.7)$$

- $ \hat{\mathbf{r}} $ is always pointing radially outwards  
- $ q $ can be +ve/-ve

**Example 21.6 P. 721**

A point charge \( q = -0.8 \, \text{nC} \) placed at the origin. The electric field at \( x = 1.2 \, \text{m} \) and \( y = -1.6 \, \text{m} \) is:

**EXECUTE:** The distance from \( S \) to \( P \) is 
\[
r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = \sqrt{(1.2 \, \text{m})^2 + (-1.6 \, \text{m})^2} = 2.0 \, \text{m}
\]

The unit vector \( \hat{r} \) is then
\[
\hat{r} = \frac{\mathbf{r}}{r} = \frac{x \hat{i} + y \hat{j}}{r} = \frac{(1.2 \, \text{m}) \hat{i} + (-1.6 \, \text{m}) \hat{j}}{2.0 \, \text{m}} = (0.60 \hat{i} - 0.80 \hat{j})
\]

Then, from Eq. (21.7),
\[
\mathbf{E} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r^2}
\]
\[
= \left(9.0 \times 10^9 \, \text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{C}^2\right)\left(-8.0 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{C}\right)\left(\frac{1}{(2.0 \, \text{m})^2}\right)
\]
\[
= (-11 \, \text{N/C}) \hat{i} + (14 \, \text{N/C}) \hat{j}
\]
